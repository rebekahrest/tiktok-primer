TikTok Ethics Primer

Data Security

Data Collected


TikTok collects information when a user creates an account or uses the platform. It collects information that users choose to provide, from third parties, and automatically.

Information that users choose to provide includes:1

  • Name, age, email, and phone number
  • Payment information, such as credit card numbers
  • Phone and social network contacts

Information obtained from third parties includes:2

  • Contacts and usage of social networks including Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and Google if used as login services
  • Advertising-related information from analytics and data providers
  • Information from publicly available sources

Information automatically collected includes: 3

  • Device information, including IP address, time zone, and device model
  • GPS location
  • Usage information such as viewed content
  • Identification of objects, scenery, and audio in videos that users upload
  • Content of messages sent through the platform

How Data is Used and Shared

Data is used by TikTok in a variety of ways, mainly for content personalization, customization of advertisements, platform functionality, and internal operations such as data analysis and improvement.4 Notably, TikTok does not sell personal information to third parties, but they still share information with other service providers and business partners. Data is shared for purposes including payment processing, technical support, and advertising and analytics.5

Analysis of Data Collection and Use

Some information TikTok collects appears to be reasonable in order for the app to function. If viewers want to use the app, they must give up some information since the recommendation algorithm relies on users’ viewing patterns to suggest content that is relevant to them. Information such as phone contacts are shared by choice in order to connect with friends on the platform. Payment information is needed to make in-app purchases, so it is reasonable for TikTok to collect it when necessary, although in data breaches this information is more sensitive.

The fact that TikTok obtains and shares information with third parties is concerning as unknown parties could be learning information without a user’s knowledge or consent. The user loses control over the scope in which their data is shared, and by extension control over their public image. Selectively revealing information is how a person shapes the way they are perceived, so this practice undermines a person’s presentation of their identity. Even though TikTok does not outright sell data, it is used by advertising companies who pay TikTok to use the platform, so the company still benefits from the commodification of data.

Extensive sharing of data also poses a safety risk. People participating in protests could be in danger from the government if their location data was revealed, and criminals could use this data to commit physical harm. The most invasive data collected, including identification of objects in videos and messaging content, poses risks to a person’s privacy. Besides intimate details about a person being used by governments for surveillance, TikTok holds a powerful position over users through its access to their messages and the people and objects that appear in their videos. By gaining insight into people’s private spaces and personal thoughts, the company has a strong capability to influence people without their knowledge if it knows what makes them “tick.”

Data Security Risks

TikTok also has data security risks of which potential users should be aware. TikTok is owned by a Chinese company called ByteDance. The head of the FBI believes this poses national security issues, warning that the Chinese government could use the app to influence American users or control their devices.6 The Chinese government could control the recommendation algorithm to influence users based on the news it promotes or censors. They could also control the data collection and software on users’ devices for surveillance and espionage purposes. These measures are possible because Chinese law requires private companies operating within the country to share their data with the government upon request, so the FBI is rightly concerned.7

Despite the current risks, the situation may change soon as TikTok is taking privacy concerns seriously. In 2022 the app moved to route US user traffic through Oracle, based in the US, which would monitor content recommendations.8 Negotiations between the US government and TikTok are in progress to address security risks, while other skeptical lawmakers have proposed legislation to ban TikTok.9 Until an agreement is passed to mitigate security risks, however, it is important for users to be aware of the potential for influence operations and data compromisation on the app.

Prevention Measures

Potential users can limit privacy risks through several settings changes. Users can choose not to share their contacts and turn off ad personalization, which limits the information TikTok can use in targeted ads. They can also make their profiles anonymous by signing up with a new email address instead of their personal ones so that their account is not linked with a third party. Users can also not like or comment on videos, or use TikTok without an account, which significantly limits personalization data from being collected.
