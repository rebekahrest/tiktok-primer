TikTok Ethics Primer

Filter Bubbles


One risk of using TikTok and other social media is that it tends to isolate users in “filter bubbles.” A filter bubble is created by personalization algorithms when they exclusively show users content that they like and agree with. Over time as the algorithm learns more about a user’s preferences, it shows them more content expressing viewpoints similar to theirs and omits information that they have not expressed interest in. As a result, people may become uninformed about current events because they only consume one-sided viewpoints and are shielded from content that they don’t agree or engage with. People’s opinions tend to become increasingly polarized when only exposed to concurring viewpoints, and they become more hostile to those who disagree. As a result, public discourse suffers when people are dismissive of others’ opinions and no one has a comprehensive understanding of the truth.

Interestingly, TikTok admits that filter bubbles are a problem with recommendation engines. One way it tries to address this is diversifying recommendations by showing videos that are not directly related to a viewer’s interests, which gives them opportunities to discover new perspectives.1 The app also avoids showing two videos in a row by the same creator. Still, these measures may not be enough to eradicate filter bubbles, and users should be wary of their effects.
